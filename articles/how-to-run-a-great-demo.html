How to run a demo that actually moves a deal

Jason Landers · March 2026 · 7 min read

The classic enterprise demo is 90 minutes of features organized by product module, delivered in the order the product team shipped them. It covers everything. It moves nothing.

The best demos I've seen — and the ones I try to coach my team toward — are structured around a single narrative. They connect a specific customer problem to a specific set of capabilities, in a sequence that builds toward a moment where the customer can see themselves using the product. That's the goal: not feature coverage, but recognition.

The failure mode everyone knows

Ask any enterprise buyer about demos and you'll hear the same things. Too long. Too generic. Too much product, not enough problem. The SE clearly didn't adapt the standard deck. There was no clear reason why any of it mattered for this company specifically.

This happens for reasons that are understandable but not acceptable. SEs are stretched across too many deals. Discovery was shallow so there's not enough signal to customize. The safe play feels like covering more ground — if you show everything, something will land. But that logic is backwards. When you show everything, nothing stands out. The customer walks away remembering the longest 90 minutes of their quarter.

Structure that works

The structure I come back to is simple: problem, capability, proof, next step.

Problem. Start by naming the specific problem the customer told you about in discovery. Not "organizations like yours often struggle with X." The actual thing this company said in the meeting two weeks ago. If you can quote it, do. "You mentioned last time that by the time your on-call team knows there's an incident, three teams have already been affected. I want to show you how we address that."

Capability. Show — don't describe — the specific capability that addresses that problem. This is where you get in the product. Keep it tight. One or two workflows, not a tour of the entire platform.

Proof. After you show the capability, connect it to an outcome. This can be a customer story, a data point, or a reference to something the customer told you they're trying to achieve. "A team with a similar environment told us this cut their mean time to resolution by about 40%."

Next step. Every demo segment should have a clear next step embedded in it — not just at the end of the meeting. "Does this address what you were describing? If so, the next thing I want to show you is how that translates to the experience for the team lead who's not on call."

The check-in discipline

The worst thing that can happen in a demo is 45 minutes of silent nodding followed by "interesting, we'll think about it." You don't learn anything. You can't adapt. You walk out with no signal about what landed.

The antidote is a deliberate check-in cadence. After each major section, ask something specific: "Does that match how your environment works?" "Is this the kind of workflow your team would actually use?" "What questions does that raise?" These aren't filler questions — they're the mechanism that turns a presentation into a conversation and gives you real-time signal about where to go deeper.

The SEs who do this well develop a sense for when the room is engaged versus when it's politely watching the clock. Reading that signal and adapting — slowing down when something lands, cutting a section when it's clearly not relevant — is one of the highest-leverage skills in the demo room.

Length and pacing

Sixty minutes is better than ninety. Forty-five is often better than sixty. The constraint forces discipline. If you only have 45 minutes, you cannot show everything — which means you have to decide what matters most. That decision is the demo. Everything else is just navigating the UI.

I've seen SEs walk out of 40-minute demos with champions who were fully sold and ready to move to a POV. I've seen 90-minute demos where everyone was technically impressive and nothing moved. Depth beats breadth, every time.

What changes with AI-assisted prep

AI pre-call tools have made demo prep faster and better. An SE who walks into a demo with an AI-generated brief covering the customer's current environment, their stated priorities from the last call, and relevant customer stories from the same vertical is starting from a much stronger position than one who didn't prepare.

The customization that used to take two hours before a major demo can now happen in 20 minutes if the data is in place. That's a real and meaningful change. But it only matters if the SE uses that time to actually customize — to think through which sections to cut, which problems to lead with, and what the room needs to see.

AI is lowering the floor on demo prep. The ceiling is still the SE's judgment.

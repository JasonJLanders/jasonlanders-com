Why this report is built the way it is
The radar chart is the point, not the score.
An SE who is strong at AI-assisted call prep but never touches it for competitive research has a completely different gap than one who is the reverse. A single number flattens that. The shape of the radar tells you where to focus. The score just tells you roughly where you are overall.
Manager mode is its own assessment, not a reworded version of the IC questions.
The IC questions ask what you know and do. The manager questions ask what you've built: onboarding, shared tooling, coaching, measurement, culture. An SE manager who says "my team uses AI" but can't describe how they enabled or measured it hasn't built an AI-ready team. They've hired people who figured it out on their own.
"The Selective User" means more than 61%.
Stage names exist because a percentage in isolation is hard to act on. "The Selective User" tells you something specific: AI is in your toolkit but not your system. You use it in a few workflows and leave value on the table in others. The stage tells you what the next level looks like -- which a number doesn't.
The hours figure is meant to make the cost feel real.
The estimates are directional, not precise. The intent is to make "I could be getting more out of AI" concrete enough that it changes how you think about investing in the habit. Abstract potential is easy to defer. Hours per week is harder to ignore.
Three actions, tied to your lowest dimension specifically.
If you score lowest on POV writing, you get POV-specific prompting advice -- not a generic reminder that AI can help with writing. The next steps are written for the actual gap, not for a generalized SE who might have any gap. That's the only way they're useful.

SE metrics that actually matter

Jason Landers · March 2026 · 7 min read

Most SE teams are measured on things that are easy to track rather than things that actually matter. Activity counts. Demos delivered. Hours logged in calls. These are visibility metrics, not performance metrics. They tell you the team is busy. They tell you almost nothing about whether the team is winning.

Building a measurement framework that drives the right behaviors — and that makes the case for SE investment to leadership — is harder than it looks. But it's one of the most important things an SE leader can do for their team.

The attribution problem

The core difficulty with SE metrics is attribution. An SE influences a deal but doesn't close it. When a deal closes, the AE gets the commission and the quota credit. When a deal is lost, the cause is usually murky — was it price, product, competition, champion loss, or an SE who didn't build the technical case well enough?

This blurriness has led a lot of SE orgs to give up on performance measurement and default to counting things that are easy to count. That's understandable but wrong. The fact that attribution is hard doesn't mean the SE's contribution can't be measured — it means you have to be deliberate about what you measure and why.

The metrics worth tracking

Technical win rate. When the SE recommends a technical win — meaning the product was validated as the right technical choice during evaluation — what percentage of those deals close? This is the strongest direct signal of SE effectiveness. High technical win rate with poor overall win rate points to commercial or champion issues. Low technical win rate points to SE execution problems.

POV conversion rate. What percentage of POVs convert to closed-won deals? And as a leading indicator: what percentage of POVs are completed on time and on scope? A POV that drags for months without closure is usually a signal that something went wrong in the setup — unclear success criteria, wrong champion, misaligned expectations. Tracking POV conversion by SE helps surface who is setting evaluations up well and who isn't.

SE coverage ratio. How many active opportunities does each SE have? The right number varies by deal complexity and segment, but a ratio that's too high means SEs are stretched thin and prioritizing by gut feel rather than strategic allocation. Tracking this over time reveals whether the team is scaling or quietly degrading under load.

Customer feedback. After major evaluations — whether won or lost — soliciting direct feedback from the customer about the SE experience is underused and valuable. Not NPS as a vanity number, but specific questions: Did the SE understand your environment? Did they address your use case? Would you work with them again? This feedback is often more actionable than any CRM data.

What not to measure

Activity counts. Demos per week, calls per month, responses per day — these measure whether someone is showing up, not whether they're doing the right things. Worse, optimizing for activity counts creates perverse incentives: SEs who schedule too many demos to look busy, who rush through discovery to get to the presentation, who follow up relentlessly regardless of whether the customer is ready.

Individual bookings credit. When SEs are measured against a booking number they share with their AE, the incentive is to focus on deals that are closing fast regardless of whether SE involvement is actually needed. The strategic work — investing early in relationships, rebuilding deals that are off-track, spending time on large but slow-moving accounts — gets deprioritized in favor of anything that will book this quarter.

Making the case to leadership

SE leaders often struggle to make the business case for their team's investment. The natural move is to point at pipeline and bookings and claim partial credit. That argument is hard to make cleanly and easy to discount.

A more credible approach: own a set of metrics that you can actually control and present them consistently over time. Technical win rate. POV conversion. Time to technical win. These tell a coherent story about the SE team's contribution without requiring disputed attribution. When those numbers improve, you can show causation rather than correlation.

The SE leaders who build organizational credibility are the ones who bring data about what they own, speak honestly about what they don't control, and make specific recommendations rather than just reporting results. That combination — measurement, honesty, and judgment — is what earns the budget and headcount to keep growing.
